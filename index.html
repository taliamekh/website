How It Works

DeskClaw — Giving AI a Body
Raspberry Pi 5Arduino YOLO113D Printing C++AI AgentsOpen CVWebsocketsWhisperElevenLabs APIPython
🏆 Best Hardware Hack · GenAI Genisis 🏆 Overall Finalist · GenAI Genisis

DeskClaw is an autonomous robot assistant designed to bridge the gap between digital intelligence and physical labor. While traditional AI agents are confined to screens, DeskClaw provides a functional "body" to assist engineers and makers in the workshop when their hands are tied. The system acts as a real-time collaborator; you can vocalize design challenges to bounce ideas off the AI or ask for a second opinion on mechanical problems as it observes your workspace. By combining advanced computer vision with voice-activated intelligence, DeskClaw transforms from a simple tool into an active participant in the creative process.

How it Works
  • Body: Tracked rover chassis driven by Raspberry Pi 5 and L289N motor driver.
  • Arm: Fully 3D-printed 6-axis robotic arm with Arduino servo control and inverse kinematics for precision positioning. Ultrasonic sensors for obstacle detection.
  • Brain: Gemini 2.5 Flash for scene reasoning · Whisper ASR for voice input · ElevenLabs for speech output.
  • Vision: Dual-camera system running YOLO11 for real-time object detection and spatial awareness.
Personal Contributions
  • Conceived the concept, designed and 3D-printed all physical components, planned and soldered all circuitry.
  • Diagnosed and resolved motor power distribution failure hours before the submission deadline.
  • Wrote robotic arm firmware in C++ on Arduino, learning and implementing inverse kinematics within the hackathon window.
Challenges and Next steps
  • Transitioned from a direct solder configuration to an external power supply and breadboard distribution to meet high current demands and ensure stable torque.
  • Developed and implemented robotic arm control logic and firmware within the last couple hours of the competition.
  • Navigated emerging OpenClaw documentation through manual troubleshooting and independent study, as the library was too recent for standard AI/LLM assistance.
  • Next Steps: Synchronize all DeskClaw functions to transition from individual operation to a fully integrated, unified robotic system.

Water Filter System & No Flow System
Environmental Engineering Filtration Design Materials Science
🏆 1st · Engineering Without Borders

The 2026 Sustainable Design Competition (SDC) was a 5-hour engineering challenge where teams were presented with a surprise environmental scenario. The prompt involved a 20,000-liter industrial fertilizer spill near a northern Canadian community, threatening a Significant Groundwater Recharge Zone (SGRA) and a local freshwater spring. We were tasked with designing and assembling two distinct subsurface columns to remediate the site:

  • Subsurface Column A: A filtration system designed to decontaminate water while allowing it to flow into the aquifer.
  • Subsurface Column B: A "No-Flow" barrier designed to completely block contaminated runoff from entering sensitive areas.
Column A — Filtration & Treatment (Top to botom)
  • Drainage Gravel:Serves as a primary physical barrier to block large debris.
  • Sand: Filters out finer particles.
  • Sand + Biochar:Implemented as a protective buffer. Biochar's efficacy is maximized when protected from direct contact with heavy sediment/soil by this transition layer.
  • Biochar: Functions via adsorption, utilizing a high surface area and extensive pore structure to trap chemical pollutants and volatile organic compounds
  • GAC (Granular Activated Carbon): Acts through a process called absorptions, where the carbons large surface area + large pores absorb/trap contaminents and polutants.
  • Resin: A specialized cation exchange resin used to reduce electrical conductivity by stripping hardness ions, such as Calcium, Magnesium, and various dissolved salts.
  • Green Sand: Utilized to deal with any remaining ions from the resin filteration, by taking the disolved ions and turning them into solids that remain trapped in the filteration media.
  • Sand: Provides a stable, structural foundation for the upper filtration layers to prevent shifting.
  • Cotton Balls: Presoaked and used to act as a final purification layer, ensuring high clarity output without absorbing and retaining water from the flow.
  • Mesh Cloth: Acts as a containment barrier to prevent cotton fibers or media from exiting the column with the treated water.
Column B — Flow Prevention (Top to Bottom)
  • Gravel: Dissipates the kinetic energy of incoming water to prevent erosion of the underlying clay barrier.
  • Sand: Facilitates even water distribution to prevent localized pooling and hydrostatic pressure on the clay layer.
  • Clay layer: Serves as the primary impermeable hydraulic barrier to halt the downward migration of water.
  • 6× Sponges: Failsafe for if water were to manage to get past the initial barrier.
  • Hydrophobic cloth: Final fail safe covering the hole at the bottom of the column
Personal Contribution
  • Researched the provided materials and developed the specific layering sequences for both the treatment and no-flow columns.
  • Guided teammates in building the columns and simulated material interactions by carving test holes into plastic cups to verify flow behavior.
Challenges
  • AI usage was strictly prohibited, requiring us to rely on manual research of the provided material within the limited design window.
  • Unable to test our filter with the actual contaminant solution until final judging, meaning the design relied entirely on theoretical material interactions.
Results
  • Original solution metrics: Turbidity (NTU) = 1.67 , Absorbance = 0.724, Electrical Conductivity(EV) = 4.4
  • After filtering: Turbidity (NTU) = 0.5 , Absorbance = 0.009, Electrical Conductivity(EV) = 2.0
  • Only team to successfully reduce turbidity and electrical conductivity compared to the original solution metrics
